Long commutes are stealing student sleep — what the data shows

A 2026 study of 431 Malaysian university students found that lengthy commutes correlate with lower grades, higher sweet-drink consumption, and less sleep. Here is why the journey to campus matters for health.

By The Wellness Post campus desk · June 22, 2026

KUALA LUMPUR: For students at Malaysian universities — particularly those in the Klang Valley — the daily commute is not merely an inconvenience. It is a structural factor that shapes when they eat, how much they sleep, and whether they have the energy to exercise or study effectively. A 2026 cross-sectional study of 431 Malaysian university students aged 18 to 25, published in JoVE, quantified these links for the first time: longer commute times were associated with lower CGPA, higher consumption of sweetened beverages, and more irregular meal patterns.

The study, conducted across multiple Malaysian universities, found that students who commuted more than 90 minutes per day were significantly more likely to skip breakfast, rely on packaged snacks between classes, and report feeling tired during lectures. The mechanism is straightforward: when a student wakes up at 6 a.m. to beat traffic, arrives on campus by 8 a.m., attends classes until 4 p.m., and then faces another 90-minute journey home, the window for preparing food, exercising, and winding down before sleep shrinks dramatically.

Nature as an antidote: what UC Irvine found

In a separate but related development, researchers at the University of California, Irvine, published a study in IOP Science in May 2026 examining the effects of nature wellness stations — designated green spaces on campus where students could sit, breathe, and disconnect for 10 to 15 minutes. The results were striking: 84 per cent of participants reported a positive emotional shift after brief nature exposure, with stress reduction being the most commonly cited benefit.

The intervention was intentionally low-cost and low-barrier. The stations required no staffing, no appointments, and no special equipment — just benches, plants, and signage encouraging students to pause. For Malaysian campuses with existing green spaces — and most have them — the model is directly transferable. Universiti Malaya's Rimba Ilmu, UKM's forest reserve, and the landscaped gardens at XMUM and Sunway University are already suited to this purpose; what is missing is the explicit framing of these spaces as wellness resources.

What universities can do

The commute problem does not have a single solution — it requires changes in housing policy, public transport, and campus scheduling. But there are steps universities can take in the short term:

Staggered class schedules. Allowing students to choose between morning and afternoon streams for core subjects reduces peak-hour travel and distributes campus density more evenly. Several Malaysian private universities already offer flexible timetabling; expanding this to public universities would benefit commuter students disproportionately.

On-campus meal subsidies. When students skip breakfast because they are rushing to class, the campus canteen becomes their first meal of the day — often at 11 a.m. or later. Subsidised healthy meal options, particularly in the morning, could reduce reliance on vending-machine snacks and sweetened drinks.

Designated rest and nature zones. Following UC Irvine's model, universities can designate existing green spaces as wellness zones with simple signage, benches, and shade — no construction budget required. The key is naming them: a bench under a tree is just a bench; a bench under a tree with a sign that says "Wellness Station — pause here for 10 minutes" becomes an invitation.

"Commute stress is not just about time lost. It is about meals skipped, sleep shortened, and the mental load of navigating traffic before a 9 a.m. exam. These are health issues, not just logistical ones." — Adapted from JoVE cross-sectional study discussion (2026)

The 2026 study's authors recommend that university health programmes address not only diet and exercise but also the structural factors — commute burden, class scheduling, and access to restorative green space — that make healthy choices harder for some students than others. For the 431 students in the study, the takeaway was clear: the journey to campus matters as much for health as what happens once you arrive.
