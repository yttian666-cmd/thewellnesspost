Liver Health

Foods to Avoid for a Healthier Liver

Your liver processes everything you eat and drink. Certain foods — especially those high in added sugars, unhealthy fats, and excess salt — can contribute to fatty liver disease over time.

Curated from Verywell Health · June 2026

Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is the most common liver condition worldwide, affecting an estimated 25 per cent of the global population. It occurs when excess fat accumulates in liver cells — not from alcohol, but primarily from diet. While genetics play a role, certain foods and eating patterns are strongly linked to liver fat accumulation. Reducing or avoiding the following foods can support liver health.

Added Sugars and Sugar-Sweetened Beverages

This is the single most important dietary change for liver health. When you consume more sugar than your body can immediately use for energy, the liver converts the excess into fat through a process called de novo lipogenesis. Fructose — the sugar in high-fructose corn syrup and table sugar — is particularly efficient at driving this process. Soft drinks, sweetened teas, fruit juices with added sugar, and even seemingly healthy smoothies with multiple servings of fruit and honey can contribute. The liver does not distinguish between "natural" sugar and added sugar when it comes to fat storage — excess is excess.

Fried and Ultra-Processed Foods

Foods fried in industrial seed oils — chips, fried chicken, doughnuts, packaged baked goods — are high in inflammatory omega-6 fatty acids and often contain trans fats. Both are associated with increased liver inflammation and fat accumulation. A 2023 study published in Clinical Gastroenterology and Hepatology found that people who consumed the most ultra-processed foods had a significantly higher risk of developing NAFLD.

Excess Salt

High sodium intake does not directly cause fatty liver, but it contributes to fluid retention and can worsen portal hypertension in people who already have liver damage. Processed meats, canned soups, instant noodles, and restaurant meals are the biggest contributors to excess sodium in most diets.

Refined Carbohydrates

White bread, white rice, pasta made from refined flour, and most breakfast cereals are rapidly digested into glucose, spiking blood sugar and insulin. Chronic high insulin levels promote fat storage in the liver. Swapping refined grains for whole grains — brown rice, oats, quinoa, whole-wheat bread — slows digestion and reduces the insulin spike.

Excess Alcohol

While NAFLD is defined as fatty liver occurring in the absence of significant alcohol consumption, alcohol is still directly toxic to liver cells. For people who already have NAFLD or elevated liver enzymes, even moderate drinking can compound the damage.
