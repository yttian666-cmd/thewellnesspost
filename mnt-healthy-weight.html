How much should you weigh for your height and age?

Medical News Today explains why no single number can define a healthy body size, and why BMI should be read as a screening tool rather than a final answer.

Curated from Medical News Today · Updated 2025

Tools such as body mass index, waist-to-hip ratio and waist-to-height ratio can help people start a conversation about health risks, but they do not describe the whole person. Muscle mass, fat distribution, ethnicity, medical history and daily activity all affect what a healthy range means.

What readers should know

BMI categories can help identify possible underweight, healthy range, overweight or obesity in adults, but BMI does not directly measure body fat. It may misclassify athletes, people with high muscle mass, older adults and people from different ethnic backgrounds.

Waist measurements can add useful context because abdominal fat is linked with metabolic and cardiovascular risk. Even then, the article stresses that body size tools should be considered alongside blood pressure, blood sugar, cholesterol, diet quality, sleep, movement and medical advice.

A healthier way to use the numbers

For young readers, the practical lesson is not to chase one exact weight. Use measurements as prompts: Are you eating regularly? Moving most days? Sleeping enough? Managing stress? If the answer is no, those habits may matter more than a single calculator result.
