Tap Water May Be Better Than Mineral Water When Taking Pills, Study Finds

Alkaline mineral waters can break down gastro-resistant drug coatings within minutes, potentially causing medications to be released in the wrong part of the digestive tract.

Curated from Medical News Today · June 2026

If you reach for a bottle of mineral water to swallow your morning medication, you may want to reconsider. A study published in Pharmaceutics examined how 22 different beverages affect the release of oral medications and found that alkaline mineral waters can cause gastro-resistant (enteric) coatings to break down prematurely — potentially within just five minutes of exposure.

Why Mineral Water Can Interfere

Many medications — particularly those for acid reflux, certain pain relievers, and some antibiotics — are coated with a gastro-resistant layer designed to survive stomach acid and release the drug in the intestines. This coating is pH-sensitive: it stays intact in acidic environments and dissolves in more neutral or alkaline conditions. Alkaline mineral water, with its higher pH, can trigger that dissolution before the pill even reaches the stomach.

The researchers found that when gastro-resistant tablets were soaked in certain mineral waters, more than 90 per cent of the active pharmaceutical ingredients were released after just five minutes. The brands Salvus and Peridot showed the strongest effects, with coatings failing within 5 to 15 minutes — meaning the drug could be released in the esophagus or stomach rather than the intestines where it is meant to be absorbed.

What to Drink Instead

The study's authors recommend plain tap or filtered water as the safest and most reliable liquid for swallowing pills. Tap water has a neutral to slightly acidic pH that does not interfere with drug coatings. Only 8.7 per cent of oral medications assessed in the study came with clear written instructions about which liquid to use — a gap the researchers say should be addressed by manufacturers and pharmacists.

For elderly people and those with swallowing difficulties — an estimated 300,000 to 600,000 people in the United States alone — the choice of beverage for taking medication is especially important, as they are more likely to take multiple coated medications daily and may be more susceptible to adverse effects from improper drug release.
