Supplements You Should Never Take With Milk

Milk is rich in calcium and casein protein — both of which can bind to certain minerals in the digestive tract, preventing proper absorption and reducing supplement effectiveness.

Curated from Verywell Health · June 2026

If you take your morning supplements with a glass of milk, you may want to reconsider. According to health experts, milk's calcium and casein content can significantly interfere with how your body absorbs several important minerals. Here are the five supplements you should never combine with dairy.

1. Iron Supplements

This is the most well-documented and clinically significant interaction. Calcium in milk directly competes with iron for absorption in the gastrointestinal tract. Even small amounts of dairy — cream in your coffee, cheese on a sandwich — can reduce how much iron your body actually uses. Take iron with water and a source of vitamin C (such as orange juice) to boost absorption. Leave at least two hours between iron supplements and any dairy.

2. Zinc Supplements

Calcium and zinc compete for the same absorption pathways in the small intestine. Taking a zinc supplement with milk can significantly reduce how much zinc reaches your bloodstream — problematic given zinc's critical role in immune function, wound healing, and protein synthesis. Take zinc with water and a protein-containing snack instead.

3. Magnesium Supplements

While the evidence is slightly less definitive than for iron and zinc, calcium from milk can still interfere with magnesium absorption. The ratio matters — a high calcium intake from dairy combined with a magnesium supplement means magnesium is likely to lose the absorption competition. Take magnesium at a different time of day than when you consume dairy.

4. Calcium Supplements (Yes, Really)

It sounds counterintuitive, but taking a calcium supplement together with milk can overload your system. Your body can only absorb a limited amount of calcium at one time. Excess intake in a single sitting may increase the risk of kidney stones and interfere with absorption of other minerals. Spread calcium intake throughout the day rather than concentrating it.

5. Multivitamins

Multivitamins contain a mix of minerals — iron, zinc, magnesium — that the calcium in milk can interfere with simultaneously. While the fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) in a multi may actually benefit from milk's fat content, the mineral-blocking effect generally outweighs this modest benefit. Take most supplements with plain water.
