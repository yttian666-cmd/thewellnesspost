Students Use Games to Expose Hidden Sugar in Everyday Drinks

At Xiamen University Malaysia, a student-led campaign called "Think Before You Drink" turned sugar labels into interactive games — helping young Malaysians see what is really in their cup, one teaspoon at a time.

By Hui Meizhe, Tian Yuting, Wang Fanghan and Zhang Bihui · Sepang · June 12, 2026

Original ASG3 video: Think Before You Drink — full event coverage. Student organisers explain the games, the message, and why sugar awareness matters.

SEPANG: XMUM students held a community service event, Think Before You Drink, on June 12 to educate students on maintaining health through daily drink choices.

The programme was organised by first-year students at the A3 Ground Floor and involved games which shed light on the secret sugar in drinks enjoyed by most students.

According to the 2024 National Health and Morbidity Survey, the average Malaysian teenager consumes 11.5 teaspoons of sugar in a day, out of which almost half is from beverages.

A report by Discover Public Health published in 2026 revealed that 86 per cent of young adults were aware that excessive consumption of sugar is unhealthy, but only 17 per cent thought it was an issue for themselves.

"As long as students become more aware of the sugar content in their drinks and start making more informed choices, we consider the event meaningful." — Joyce Ng, event organiser

Joyce Ng, one of the organisers, explained that they decided to organise the activity due to the lack of awareness among the students about what they are actually consuming.

She further said that the majority of students underestimate the sugar in common beverages, and even for those who regularly consume milk tea, the estimated figure was much less than the actual sugar content.

She said many participants stayed at the booth longer than expected, discussing the sugar content of different drinks and comparing their guesses with friends.

"The aim of our activities is obvious. We want to raise awareness among XMUM students about the sugar content in their daily drinks," she said, adding that they hoped that once students knew the truth, they would think twice before ordering.

The most popular activity, Guess the Sugar, asked participants to estimate the sugar content of their favourite drinks. For instance, a 16-ounce bubble tea drink has 38 grams of sugar, which is already close to the recommended upper limit of daily sugar intake for an adult.

However, while going through the activity, many students claimed that they do not consider sugar when ordering a drink.

Cai Zhuyi, a student interviewed at the booth, said the amount of sugar had never crossed her mind. "I never really thought about how much sugar was in it. I usually choose drinks based on what I feel like having that day," she said.

She added that she buys drinks about four times a week and often sees them as a quick refreshment after class.

"The second activity was Sweet Karma. It used a board game format where students rolled dice and answered questions about their daily eating and drinking habits," Ng said. Based on their scores, the students received one of four results: Healthy, Average, Needs Improvement, or Unhealthy. "Each result came with specific advice on how to improve their lifestyle," she added.

"The third activity was Build Your Drink. Students used a computer to customise a virtual drink by choosing a base, flavour, and topping. They then guessed the total sugar content of the drink they had just made," she said.

Zhang Ruixuan, who took part in the event, said the information caught her off guard. "The amount was far higher than I expected," she said. She also said Build Your Drink impressed her the most. "I originally thought it was a normal combination, but the final calculation of the sugar was much higher than expected."

Zhang added that she was particularly surprised to learn that some drinks that appear healthy, such as fruit tea, can still contain large amounts of sugar. She said that through the activities, she understood that making healthier decisions would not imply abstaining from her favourite beverages completely.

Dr Barakatun Nisak Mohd Yusof, a nutrition expert from Universiti Putra Malaysia, said that sugary drinks are one of the main reasons young people face risks of weight gain, diabetes, and heart disease.

While health practitioners recommend that no more than 25 grams of sugar be consumed per day by adults, one serving of carbonated drinks contains roughly 35 grams of sugar.

According to a study conducted nationally in 2017, more than one out of three Malaysian youths aged 13 to 17 had become habitual consumers of carbonated beverages daily.

Malaysia has been taxing sugar-containing drinks since 2019, but after increasing the taxation rates to 90 sen per litre of the beverage in January 2025, the government has collected over RM521 million. This measure expanded on the sugar tax introduced in 2019, which aimed to address the country's alarming rates of diabetes and obesity.

The government has allocated RM25 million from sugar tax collections to fund public health campaigns and diabetes-related medical support, according to the Ministry of Finance.

A 2024 report by the Ministry of Health noted that most youths who have cut back on their consumption of sugary beverages have done so based on health reasons and not because of prices.

However, Ng conceded that it would be impossible to reverse the trend overnight. "As long as students become more aware of the sugar content in their drinks and start making more informed choices, we consider the event meaningful," she said.

The event only lasted one day, but for the students who took part, it may change how they think the next time they order a drink.
